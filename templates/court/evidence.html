Unrelaible Evidence


Police cannot use evidence that is known to be unreliable.   Unreliable evidence includes confessions which which were obtained through pomises or threats.   It also includes statements about what a person heard someone else say and identifications from lineups.   (Yes lineups, popular in movies, have caused many wrongful convictions and are not allowed, you have to show suspects sequentially).   There are many other examples of unreliable evidence that would take an entire textbook to go over.


Unconstitutional Evidence


Police cannot use evidence if it was obtained in an unconstitutional way.   For example, if the police search you without having a good reason, they cannot use what they find against you in court.   This is because the constitution doesn't allow the police to arbitrarily search people.


Unconstitutional evidence does not automatically become unusable.   To get rid of the evidence, you must file a constitutional application at least 30 days before the trial date.   The application must state what evidence you want to get rid of and the constitutional basis for getting rid of it.
